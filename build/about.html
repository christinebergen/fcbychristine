About Christine

My name is Christine, it is the name I chose when I was 11. I knew I was a caregiver from very early on.

In my 20's I was trained by an RN to help care for her complex care father to provide care in his own home. I learned skills that were quite involved and complex. This helped me figure out that I liked helping people and I loved the challenges of complex care. Shortly afterwards, I went to Malaspina University College (now Vancouver Island University) and finished the Home Support Residential Care Aid course in 1997 (now called Health Care Aid) and completed in the top 5. This led me to my first job in healthcare, a group home for high medical need clients, (most of the clients had challenges with cognitive differences, physical challenges, and high seizure activity).

While working at the Group home I gained employment working with Quadreplegic and Paraplegic people living in the community. I learned a lot in those three years. I learned mostly that I need more education. So I went back to school and completed the Licensed Practical Nursing program in 2001. Immediately upon graduation I got a temporary line on the acute medical floor (floor 3) at Nanaimo Regional General Hospital for 3years. I loved this work. It was intense. The learning curve was steep and the acuity was high. After the 3year line on the third floor I continue to work all over the hospital for approximately 15 years. Rehab, Long Term Care, Emergency, Palliative, Portering, Acute Care, are all the places I worked, however, after all that time I was unable to find a permanent line. 2003 I had my first baby and in 2007 my second. Both beautiful girls. I was struggling to work as an on-call employee and as a new mom. This is when I made the decision to find other work.

On a whim I dropped off a resume at a medical clinic in my neighbourhood and surprisingly started working the next week. I loved this job. I got to provide education to patients, provide wound care and general support to the physicians and staff alike. A couple of years later I was promoted to Manager. Also, at this time our Medical Director had 2 patients that had negative outcomes due to poor Foot Care, and this affected him deeply. He and I had worked together for a few years at this point and he knew that I loved education. He graciously offered to pay for my course if I would bring the skills into the clinic in hopes to never have another unnecessary negative outcome. I agreed and was lucky enough to work with VIHA afterwards working with low income and/or complex/chronic disease patients. This was an incredible experience. In 2018 the clinic had to close due to physician shortages and I opted to go out on my own. This was the start of my independent journey.

The Next Chapter

All throughout my journey I was exposed to deaths in all of its forms, sudden, long, peaceful, and violent. I have had many conversations with medical personnel, families and friends of the dying and the dying themselves. I noticed that the people that had a lot of conversations around death and dying seemed to be more at peace in their final moments than those who hadn't. I also noticed that the more accepting of the inevitable outcome everyone involved was, the more calm and supported the dying seem to be. This has been a side note in everything I did as a nurse, as an observer and as caregiver or a person who truly cares. Along the way, I also have been blessed to have some discussions with my Foot Care Clients and realized that I was drawn to working with people around the dying process. This is my second calling and what I am working on currently. I am in the process of learning how to be an End of Life Doula.

What is an End of Life Doula? An End of Life Doula is someone who supports people in the end of life. They help with discussions around death to empower, educate and encourage all who become involved in the process. Culturally in North America death is a taboo subject and considered negatively, or bad instead of part of the human experience. The End of Life Doula does not impose preconceived notions of death and dying upon the person and their family/friends rather they empower them to figure out what is important to them and how to create an experience that is meaningful to the person who is dying and their loved ones. A person does need to be actively dying or given a life limiting diagnosis to utilize an End of Life Doula. An End of Life Doula can be a part of the planning at any point.

I look forward to continuing on this journey and hope to be able to provide this service in the near future. If you wish to learn more about End of Life Doula work please feel free to browse the following website: https://endoflifedoulaassociation.org/.
